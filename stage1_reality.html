Stage 1: Reality

What kind of motion does a projectile follow?

Imagine a cannonball fired from a fort. After the initial explosive push, it rises, slows, and falls ‚Äî all while tracing a curved path through the air.

Now consider a cricketer on the boundary throwing the ball toward the wicketkeeper, or a footballer kicking the ball across the field. In all these cases, once the object is in the air, it is under the influence of only one force: gravity.

The motion that follows is called projectile motion. The object moves in two dimensions ‚Äî horizontally and vertically ‚Äî and its path is typically a parabola. Our goal is to model this motion mathematically so we can predict the object's position at any time.
